    From exploring childhood homes to reconnecting with former loves, the ease in which we can move fluidly between our past and present selves can be surprising but simultaneously comforting. The Korean concept of “in-yun” describes the fated connections between people, sometimes over many lifetimes, and is a theme that permeates through Celine Song's debut film, Past Lives.

    In the film, Nora (Greta Lee) reconnects with her childhood friend Hae Sung (Teo Yoo) decades after she moves to Canada from South Korea, and the pair reflect on how their lives could have been had they never separated. Inspired by this reunion, one writer contemplates how we are always connected to our past selves.

    It was lockdown, and my aunt was dying. I had flown home to Virginia in a rush from London, changing my plans as soon as my mum messaged me. I can’t remember the specifics, apart from “hope is low” and “there is nothing more the doctors can do”.

    I never knew what it meant to watch someone deteriorate until I saw my aunt in the hospital. At first, her body swelled in a way that scared me. I couldn’t touch her, fearful that she would burst. Then, once the swelling stopped, she was all bones.

    Nicole Ocran

    Nicole and her aunt

    I don’t know what it was about being back home that made me feel compelled to call my childhood friend. It had been over 15 years since we’d last seen each other, so I wasn’t even sure if he still lived nearby. He was my best friend at high school, where we would fall in and out of love with each other – handwritten letters and burned CDs a testament to our feelings.

    I got his number through an acquaintance and left an awkward voicemail.

    “I’m not sure if you remember me, but…”

    Minutes later, he returned my call, sounding exactly the same as I remembered him. His voice had been too deep for him at 16 but suited the grown man he had become. We chatted excitedly after discovering he had moved to my neighbourhood and agreed to catch up over a drink.

    It felt comfortable quickly, and I felt myself slowly slip back into my younger self. It was as if I’d never left.

    Nicole Ocran

    I had lived an entire life since the last time we saw each other (moved over 3,000 miles away, had multiple careers, married, bought a house, and was now separated and selling said house) but sitting opposite each other on an unseasonably warm spring afternoon, I was once again the teenage girl who would stay up all night on the phone to him. I would ask him to tell me stories as I fell asleep, comforted just by knowing he was there.

    Maybe that was why I conjured him so easily in my mind. My whole world was changing. It felt like I was hurtling into my thirties and the stable ground I had built for myself wasn’t so stable after all. Death and divorce had me searching for something safe and familiar.

    StudioCanal

    Nora and Hae Sung in Past Lives

    The drinks flowed easily. The excitement of our reconnection and our unwillingness to allow the evening to end meant that we stayed up all night. I confessed to my diary the next day that we had slept together and I was unsure about what it meant (I often am) but that I was happy.

    We were happy, I like to think, for a while. We agreed quickly that we wanted to try and make a relationship work. After two deaths in my family, I had decided that I would move back to Virginia, wanting to salvage what was left of the life I had left there. I felt selfish being gone for so long, and now I had a long-distance relationship I had to make work.

    I don’t know if it was the distance or fear – or both – but in the end, we finished things uncomfortably over WhatsApp in the middle of the night.

    I still love him, in all the lives that I knew him. And all we can be to each other is what we were when we were our teenage selves, and that’s OK. The beauty of our connections and our relationships are in the moments we experience them. That doesn’t make them any less real. That doesn’t diminish what we felt at the time.
