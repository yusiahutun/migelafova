    Self-described 'mountain man' in 1984 killing is released

    Self-described 'mountain man' Don Nichols was released on parole Wednesday after serving 32 years for kidnapping a world-class athlete out on a training run in 1984 and also killing her would-be rescuer.

    26 Jun 2024, 00:00

    Self-described 'mountain man' Don Nichols was released on parole Wednesday after serving 32 years for kidnapping a world-class athlete out on a training run in 1984 and also killing her would-be rescuer. 

    The 86-year-old was sentenced to 85 years in prison for kidnapping biathlete Kari Swenson in the mountains near Big Sky and killing Alan Goldstein, a friend helping to search for Swenson when she failed to return home from her run.

    The vicious attack drew widespread media coverage at the time and became the subject of a made-for-TV movie.  

    Swenson, who was 22 at the time, said she was chained up during her ordeal and spotted her would-be rescuers before her abductors did. 

    Self-described 'mountain man' Don Nichols (above in 2012) was released on parole Wednesday after serving 32 years for kidnapping a world-class athlete out on a training run in 1984 and also killing her would-be rescuer

    Nichols, who must report to a parole office in Great Falls, Montana, was granted parole in April. The 1984 photo above  shows Don Nichols (right) being taken into custody with his son, Dan, near Bozeman, Montana

    She yelled at them to leave because Nichols had threatened to shoot anyone who tried to help her. 

    Nichols shot Goldstein, and Nichols' son Dan apparently accidentally shot Swenson. 

    She said she was left for dead with a 'sucking chest wound' for hours as Goldstein's body lay nearby.

    Don and Dan Nichols fled and were arrested five months later after a manhunt in the mountains of southwestern Montana.

    During the parole hearing in April, board members noted Nichols' clean record in his more than 30 years in prison and his completion of educational programs, including anger management and life skills.

    Dan (left in 1984) was sentenced to 85 years in prison for kidnapping biathlete Kari Swenson (right) in the mountains near Big Sky and killing Alan Goldstein, a friend helping to search for Swenson when she failed to return home from her run

    Don Nichols, who kidnapped Swenson to be a bride for his then 19-year-old son, told the board members they would not regret their decision and he felt bad about his crimes.

    His son, Don Nichols, was convicted of helping with the crime and released from prison in 1991. In 2013 he was sentenced to prison again in a federal drug case

    The parole board approved his release and he must report to a parole office in Great Falls. 

    He cannot go to Gallatin or Madison counties, and must wear a GPS monitor. 

    Before Don Nichols' 2012 parole hearing, Kari Swenson wrote a letter to The Bozeman Daily Chronicle calling the father and son 'crazy misfits' who chose to live apart from society and defy its laws.

    'I endured being grabbed by both wrists, hit in the face, thrown to the ground, chained to Dan, threatened with knives and guns, marched through the woods, secured like an animal to trees and spent a terrifying night chained next to Dan,' she wrote.

    She said she spent years in counseling and still has shrapnel in her chest that hurts her and brings back haunting memories of the ordeal that ended her athletic career.

    Dan Nichols was convicted of kidnapping and assault and was released from prison in 1991.

    The 1984 case involving the father and son was the subject of a 1987 made-for-TV movie 'The Abduction of Kari Swenson,' starring Tracy Pollan (right)

    But in 2013 he was sentenced in federal court to four years in prison and three years of supervised probation in a federal drug case.

    The 1984 case involving the father and son was the subject of a 1987 made-for-TV movie 'The Abduction of Kari Swenson,' starring Tracy Pollan. 

    It was also featured in an episode of Investigation Discovery's TV show 'Your Worst Nightmare.'
