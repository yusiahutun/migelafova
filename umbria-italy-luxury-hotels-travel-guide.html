    The region of Umbria is Italy’s best-kept secret. Known as the 'green heart of Italy', it’s a place of unspoilt nature, enchanting landscapes, exquisite cuisine and high culture. Not dissimilar, in fact, to its rather more famous neighbour, Tuscany – but without the many crowds. Indeed, while the rest of the country is increasingly overrun by tourists and film sets seeking their own slice of la dolce vita (see: Sicily, Venice and the Amalfi Coast), Umbria has managed to elude the spotlight almost entirely, and thereby retain its authentic rustic charm.

    That’s precisely what makes Umbria such a wonderful place to visit. It has all the UNESCO World Heritage Sites, picturesque towns, important art works, fabulous restaurants and cultural events of its glitzier counterparts, but they’re just a little further off the beaten track – and offer a satisfying sense of seeing a side to Italy that’s still relatively undiscovered. No wonder the region has become the holiday hideaway of choice for the likes of Gwyneth Paltrow, Ralph Fiennes and Colin Firth – particularly since British Airways introduced a London-Perugia leg for the spring/summer months. And with a new Six Senses hotel on the horizon, too, Umbria looks to be finally getting the attention it deserves.

    Here's our insider’s guide to enjoying the region in all its glory, from the places to eat, drink, shop and spa, to the best luxury hotels and private villas to call home.

    Where to stay in Umbria

    Reschio

    PHILIP VILE

    An ancient, magical and magnificent estate located right on the edge of the Umbria-Tuscany border, Reschio is undoubtedly the region’s foremost luxury address. When the aristocratic Bolza family happened across it in 1984, it was as rustic as they come: an overgrown, untended expanse of rural countryside dotted with long-abandoned farmhouses and a crumbling castle dating back more than 1,000 years. Visionaries that they were, however, they saw in it the perfect place for those seeking total tranquillity and seclusion of the most exclusive kind – and so, for the next few decades, they set about restoring, revitalising and reimagining Reschio, transforming it into the spectacular hideaway it is today.

    Out of respect for the history and surroundings, the estate from the outside looks much like it might have back in the day – not counting the supremely manicured grounds, striking outdoor art installations and impressive roster of cars lining the winding drive, of course. Inside, though, it’s a marvel of pure luxury: think crackling fireplaces, live piano, elegant cocktails, a palm-filled glasshouse, dramatic four-poster beds, standalone tubs, Renaissance paintings and priceless antiques at every turn. Even the furniture and lighting are completely bespoke, designed on-site by Benedikt Bolza and made by local artisans (and available to purchase for those looking to bring the Reschio aesthetic back home).

    PHILIP VILE

    With just 35 rooms, two restaurants, a glitzy bar, enchanting bath-house spa, 1,500 hectares of land to explore and everything from horse-riding and tennis to wild lake swimming on the agenda, Reschio is the kind of place one visits in order to stay firmly put – and that’s precisely the draw. A shame in regards to discovering the rest of what Umbria has to offer, admittedly, but a godsend for those looking for total privacy to completely switch off and get away from it all. It’s a combination that’s like catnip for those in the public eye, so don’t be surprised if you spot an A-lister at breakfast, or a major Hollywood actress (who just happens to own a private house on the estate) taking a dip the pool.

    BOOK A ROOM

    Vocabolo Moscatelli

    Victor Fitz, courtesy Vocabolo Moscatelli

    Vocabolo Moscatelli is the latest addition to the Umbrian luxury hotel scene, and one of the most alluring. Set within a former 12th-century listed monastery on an estate close to Umbertide, it's super intimate with just twelve bedrooms and a distinctly relaxed vibe that feels more like staying in a friend's stylish country home than a hotel. It's all down to husband-and-wife team Frederik and Catharina, the most gracious and warmly welcoming of hosts whose love of the region and passion for community is infectious – and evident in Vocabolo's design, too.

    Courtesy Vocabolo Moscatelli

    The duo worked with an impressive number of artisans, designers and artists from across the region to give their bucolic bolthole a hyper-local look and feel. The main house, for example, retains the beautiful beamed ceilings, stone fireplaces and terracotta brick work that scream Umbria, but is elevated with modern ceramics, colourful handmade tiles and striking, bold furnishings such as Lispi metal canopy beds and bespoke oversized tubs. For those with a design eye who are seeking solace, impeccable service and rural tranquillity, Vocabolo is utter bliss – and there's everything from pottery classes and truffle hunting to forest bathing and yoga on offer keep you well-occupied, too.

    BOOK A ROOM

    Aethos Saragano

    Courtesy Aethos Saragano

    If you’re drawn to the quiet solitude of rural village life but couldn’t possibly do without your usual dose of luxury, Aethos Saragano offers the best of both worlds. A modern take on the albergo diffuso (a form of hotel where guest rooms are spread out in various buildings) it’s a boutique hotel with 15 rooms and apartments scattered around a still-inhabited medieval Umbrian village, surrounded by old Roman walls, vineyards and olive groves. The streets are cobbled, the piazza is always thrumming with friendly locals, the ambience is peaceful and the views from the hilltop are spectacular ­– and while the setting is as authentic as they come, the chic interiors, attention to detail and standard of service could only be the trappings of a world-class hotel.

    Lifelong Saragano resident Chef Delfina heads up the excellent CECI restaurant, using local ingredients – including many from her own garden – to recreate family recipes passed down through generations, with a contemporary, international twist. She also runs cooking workshops for guests who want to bring their new-found appreciation of Umbrian cuisine home with them. Those with itchy feet can take part in a number of experiences, including archery, wine tours, e-bike expeditions, truffle hunting and olive oil harvesting, or opt for pure relaxation with in-room massages, meditation and dips in the pool. For a supremely spoiling experience, opt to stay in the romantic Panorama Jacuzzi Suite, that offers exactly what it says on the tin.

    BOOK A ROOM

    Hotel Brufani

    Courtesy SINA Brufani

    Holding court over the historic centre of Perugia, the five-star Hotel Brufani boasts views across the Umbria’s rolling hills on one side and the medieval city on the other. It’s the perfect base from which to explore the region’s fascinating capital, situated at the most panoramic end of its main thoroughfare, Corso Vannucci. Just a few minutes’ walk from its grand front doors, you’ll find buzzy restaurants, bars and boutiques aplenty, as well as Perugia’s iconic piazza, Cathedral and most important historical sites.

    The Brufani itself is quite the grande dame: dating back to 1884, it’s hosted everyone from the Queen Mother to Prince Albert of Monaco over the years – and while it has, naturally, been revamped a few times since, its old-world glamour continues to shine through. Rooms are bedecked in rich velvets and sumptuous silks, featuring glossy mahogany furnishings, marble bathrooms and gilded fixtures, while the underground spa boasts a fabulous heated swimming pool with a front-row view to ancient Etruscan ruins.

    BOOK A ROOM

    Borgobrufa

    Courtesy Borgobrufa

    A few days in Umbria can do wonders for the soul, but for those in need of some more hands-on help to achieve full zen, there’s Borgobrufa. It’s the region’s largest and finest spa resort, located just a 20-minute drive from Perugia but a world away from the hustle and bustle of the (admittedly very small) city. The sprawling countryside views alone are enough to melt any lingering worries away, best enjoyed whilst floating in the outdoor pool on a warm summer’s day, or, even better, from your private terrace at sunset with a glass of wine from the estate’s own vineyards in hand.

    Everything at Borgobrufa is centred around pure wellbeing, from the countryside-chic interiors, strict adults-only policy and Michelin-starred Elementi restaurant to the roster of relaxing activities ranging from picnics and yoga to guided horse and bike rides. But it’s the Nordic-inspired spa that’s the real star here – all 3,000 square metres of it, complete with salt cave, sauna, steam room and spoiling treatments using divinely-scented products from the estate.

    BOOK A ROOM

    NUN Assisi

    Courtesy NUN Assisi

    A place of pilgrimage for Catholics and culture-lovers alike, the medieval hilltop town of Assisi is an essential pit stop on any Umbrian itinerary. It’s the birthplace of Saint Francis, an important UNESCO World Heritage site and home to breath-taking Basilicas, churches, and artistic masterpieces by the likes of Giotto and Cimabue. Most spend a few hours here before heading off to explore elsewhere, but there’s something to be said for soaking up Assisi’s soothing, almost otherworldly atmosphere for a little longer…

    The best way to do so is by staying a night or two at the (rather appropriately named) NUN, a 13th-century convent turned five-star hotel with 18 minimalist suites. Perched right at the top of Assisi’s historic centre, it boasts brilliant views and a quiet spot for relaxation away from the tourist throngs, while still being an easy stroll to all the main sites. There’s much to experience within its walls, too, from the spa built into the ruins of a first-century amphitheatre, a mini museum of ancient artefacts and Roman-inspired wellness treatments, to the excellent Umbrian cuisine on offer at Benedikto restaurant.

    Private Villas

    Courtesy Le Collectionist

    Villa Oliverde

    Airbnb is all well and good for the odd low-key local stay, but if you’re after a luxury villa with all the bells and whistles, Le Collectionist is the name to know. The high-end holiday rental brand has 19 Umbrian properties in its portfolio, each hand-picked for its unique allure and sense of place – ranging from the small-but-perfectly-formed Villa Ruscello for a romantic getaway à deux, to the sprawling Villa Castella, Villa Del Lago and Villa Oliverde, which are ideal for group holidays. Each comes complete with a dedicated concierge team to take care of the logistics, who are on hand to personalise your stay with everything from exclusive local experiences to personal chefs and babysitters.

    What to see and do in Umbria

    Umbria Jazz Festival

    photo by Giancarlo Belfiore/Umbria Jazz

    For ten days every summer, the streets and historic landmarks of Perugia come alive with the joyous sounds of jazz as the city plays host to the world-renowned Umbria Jazz Festival. Everyone from B.B. King, Miles Davis, Tony Bennett and Gilberto Gil to Sting, Lady Gaga and Erykah Badu has played here over the years, with big-ticket concerts taking place at the Arena Santa Giuliana, while the town centre is filled with free gigs, events, workshops and street music from morning to late at night. 12-21 July 2024

    Fondazione Burri

    Photo by DeAgostini/Getty Images

    Immerse yourself in the life and works of Alberto Burri, the prominent, Umbria-born 20th-century visual artist, painter, sculptor and physician who inspired the Arte Povera movement. Located close to his native Città di Castello, the Burri Foundation's large museum complex has permanent displays of the artist's oeuvre and temporary exhibitions, as well as private guided tours.

    Solomeo

    Sante Castignani; courtesy Brunello Cucinelli

    You will likely have heard of the so-called 'king of cashmere', Brunello Cucinelli – but did you know that the majority of his luxury clothing is created in Umbria? In a medieval hilltop hamlet called Solomeo, to be specific, that the designer has been carefully restoring and preserving for the past few decades, and where he employs a large number of inhabitants to craft his exquisite collections. It's well worth a visit to the town for a nosy around, as well as the flagship boutique for some in-situ shopping. It's particularly special at Christmas, when the hamlet is dotted with typical nativity scenes.

    Galleria Nazionale dell'Umbria

    Photo by DeAgostini//Getty Images

    Lower part of the Tezi Altarpiece, ca 1500, by Pietro Perugino at the Galleria Nazionale dell’Umbria, Perugia

    Housed in Perugia's Palazzo dei Priori, the Galleria Nazionale dell'Umbria is an ode to the region's rich artistic and cultural heritage. Spanning the 13th – 19th centuries, it has more than 500 artworks on display including masterpieces by the likes of Piero della Francesca, Perugino and Pinturicchio.

    Wine tasting

    Courtesy Tenute Lunelli

    Tenute Lunelli’s Carapace winery in Castelbuono

    Though less well-known than those of neighbouring Tuscany, Umbria's wines are among the finest in Italy – and generally less expensive. Names to know include Sagrantino di Montefalco, Cervaro della Sala, Orvieto, Montefalco Rosso and Colli Martani. You can sample the best that the area has to offer at Perugia's Bottega del Vino wine bar, but we'd also recommend paying a visit to the Tabarrini winery and Lunelli's Castelbuono estate for an epic tasting in situ.

    Deruta

    Courtesy La Bottega Del Vasaio

    Head to the small medieval town of Deruta to pick up some beautiful, hand-painted, colourful Maiolica ceramics from one of 200 family-owned workshops that line its streets. La Bottega Del Vasaio makes some of the best, and also has a shop in Perugia if you don't manage to make it out of town.

    The great outdoors

    Courtesy Reschio

    You'll gain a good appreciation of Umbria's scenic landscapes even just by driving between its various hilltop towns – Orvieto, Spoleto, Spello, Todi and Gubbio all being important ones to tick off. But to fully immerse yourself in the region's beauty, you should spend some time in nature, whether walking in the Monte Cucco, Monte Subasio and Monti Sibillini national parks, cycling around Lake Trasimeno, exploring a nature trail on horse-back, hiking to the Marmore Waterfalls, or heading into the forest on a truffle hunt.

    Where to eat in Umbria

    Osteria a Priori

    Courtesy Osteria a Priori

    Located right in the historic centre of Perugia on Via dei Priori, this charming little restaurant is run by a husband and wife team who have been specialising in zero-kilometre, slow food since long before it became a trendy global movement. The menu is the ultimate showcase of Umbria's culinary excellence and features solely regional ingredients, such as Norcia's world-renowned salumi, Colfiorito lentils, red onion from Cannara, locally-caught wild boar and rabbit, cheeses from various micro-dairies in the Apennines, and, of course, lashings of the famous black truffle that grows here in abundance. The wine cellar is vast and extremely well-priced, so this is a good opportunity to sample some small-producer Umbrian vintages, too.

    Ada

    Courtesy Ada Restaurant

    The latest Umbrian addition to the Michelin guide is Ada, a buzzy new restaurant in Perugia where tradition meets innovation. Chef and owner Ada Stifani is the first (and only) woman in the region to be awarded a star, thanks to her creative and eye-catching dishes that are inspired by Umbria and Italy at large, but that also display a distinctly multi-cultural flair.

    Vespasia

    Courtesy Palazzo Seneca

    Norcia might be well-known for its fabulous produce and skilled artisanal butchers, but it's Vespasia that put it on the map for fine dining connoisseurs. The elegant restaurant housed within Palazzo Seneca hotel has held a Michelin star for a decade, and was recently also awarded a Michelin Green star for its dedication to sustainable gastronomy and practices.

    Luce

    Courtesy Luce Ristorante

    The storied palazzo that houses Luce, with its Renaissance-era vaulted ceilings and Etruscan stone walls, belies the contemporary cool of what lies within. Founded by the charismatic young Gori brothers, this restaurant is undoubtedly one of the most atmospheric in town; all low lights, minimalist interiors and funky plates carrying modern reimaginings of traditional Umbrian cuisine.
